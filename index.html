The Investigation

Sindh Files is a powerful cinematic project shedding light on the silent suffering of religious minorities in Pakistan.

The series explores real cases of forced conversion and child marriage through a human-centered, investigative lens. We move beyond statistics to tell the stories of stolen daughters, broken families, and a justice system that has turned a blind eye. This is not just a film; it is an archive of truth.

Rinkle Kumari

1993 — 2012 (Abducted)

Case File #001

A Symbol of
Stolen Futures

Rinkle Kumari was a 17-year-old Hindu girl from Mirpur Mathelo, Sindh. A bright student with dreams of her own, her life was irrevocably altered in February 2012.

Abducted from her home, she was forcibly converted and married to a man she did not choose. Her case reached the Supreme Court of Pakistan, becoming a watershed moment that exposed the systemic vulnerability of minority girls.

Despite her pleas in court—crying out that she wanted to return to her mother—the system failed her. She was never returned to her family.

Rinkle Kumari Case — Full Timeline

Prepared by Pakistan Institute of Labour Education and Research (PILER), December 2012

Feb 24, 2012

Abduction / Disappearance

Rinkle Kumari was abducted early morning.
11:00 am: Her father Nand Lal received a call from Mian Aslam (son of Mian Abdul Haq, aka Mian Mitho) claiming Rinkle was with him and had embraced Islam. Later in the evening, the family was informed her Nikah had been administered with Naveed Shah. Mukhi (leader) Namo Mal contacted Mitho to settle the dispute; Mitho refused. Joint protests blocked the National Highway. Police shelled protesters; markets closed; FIR lodged for kidnapping. Police refused to name Mitho and his sons; even DPO could not recover her from Mitho’s custody.

Feb 25, 2012

Civil Court Production (Ghotki)

Produced before Judge Hasan Ali Kalwar (Civil Court Ghotki). Seeing her family, she screamed and pleaded to go with her mother. The judge warned that if her parents insisted on taking her, they would be responsible for the killing of “2000 Hindus” in District Ghotki by religious extremists. Custody was given to police with orders to send her to Sukkur Women’s Police Station, but she was reportedly transported in Mitho’s son’s private car. Next hearing date set: Feb 27.

Feb 26, 2012

Threats in Custody (Sukkur)

Family met her at Women’s Police Station Sukkur. Rinkle said Mian Mitho spoke to her the previous night using a police constable’s phone. When she refused, she was manhandled and forced to speak. She reported Mitho threatened her to change her statement if she wanted to see her family alive.

Feb 27, 2012

Night Transfer, Sealed Court & Judgment

Police took Rinkle from Sukkur to Mirpur Mathelo at 2:00 am (per sources); official version says 3:30 am to Sukkur District Headquarters.
3:30 am: SHO Zulfiqar Meher called family saying hearing would be in Ghotki instead of Mirpur Mathelo; hearing eventually took place in Mirpur Mathelo.
Court area (~1 km) was sealed for Hindus; armed men present. Family members allowed were limited; later only two were allowed in: Sulachini Devi and Daya Ram at 8:30 am.
8:45 am: loud “Mubarak” sounds were heard from inside for her “embracing Islam.” Judgment declared her Muslim and married to Naveed Shah.

Feb 28, 2012

Press Conference & Attack

Her uncles held a press conference against injustice and state high-handedness. Mitho’s men allegedly stormed into her grandfather’s office and fired at him. He survived. Family shifted to Karachi due to fear.

Feb 29, 2012

Deal Attempt & Mediation Fails

Mian Mitho contacted political figures offering a “deal”: Rinkle could meet family if they accepted her religious and marital status. Family agreed only if her decision was proven free-will. A proposal to keep her 12 hours in a neutral environment was rejected. Mediation attempts (including by Khalid Soomro) failed.

March 3, 2012

Petition Filed (Sindh High Court)

Family filed petition in Sindh High Court (Karachi) challenging the Feb 27 judgment of Session Court Mirpur Mathelo.

March 6, 2012

Appeal Accepted & Custody Issues

Appeal accepted by Sindh High Court; hearing set for March 12. Rinkle remained in police custody. Allegations: police facilitated Mitho contacting her; Inspector Hajra Usman allegedly coerced her to take Mitho’s phone call; threats repeated if she changed her statement.

March 8, 2012

Supreme Court Takes Notice

Supreme Court reopened a 2007 Pakistan Hindu Council petition on forced conversions and included Rinkle’s case, along with Dr. Lata Kumari and Aasha Kumari. Next hearing fixed for March 26.

March 11, 2012

Press Conference Under Custody

A press conference was organized while she was still in police custody. Reports alleged she was dictated via Bluetooth device; cross-questioning was not allowed; she was taken away when reporters questioned. Coverage was used to shape public perception before court proceedings.

March 12, 2012

High Court Tension & Adjournment

Around two hundred men reportedly appeared outside the High Court with open display of weapons. Court kept her custody with police and closed proceedings due to Supreme Court orders to produce her on March 26. US Congressman Brad Sherman wrote to Pakistan’s President urging attention to the matter.

March 26, 2012

Supreme Court Hearing & Shelter Order

Rinkle Kumari and Dr. Lata Kumari were produced. Rinkle cried and screamed that she wanted to go with her mother. Proceedings moved in camera. Despite her statement, the court ordered both girls to be kept in Panah shelter home with restrictions on meeting anyone, to allow a “free environment.” Case adjourned to April 18.

March 27, 2012

Public Statement (CII Chairman)

Maulana Shirani, Chairman Council of Islamic Ideology, stated that if she was forcibly converted she should be sent home, and that forced conversions have no place in Islam.

April 10, 2012

Meeting Request Rejected & Defiance Statement

Naveed Shah requested Supreme Court permission to meet her; Chief Justice rejected the request stating she wanted to go with her mother. At a seminar in Islamabad, Mian Mitho stated he would not accept a Supreme Court decision if it went against him.

April 12–14, 2012

Alleged Meetings at Panah

Allegations that Naveed Shah and Mian Aslam met Rinkle multiple times in Panah. Justice Majida Razvi denied the claim and offered CCTV footage if needed. Family claimed credible information that staff were threatened to facilitate meetings.

April 17, 2012

Family & Activists’ Press Conference

Human rights activists and her family held a press conference in Islamabad stating they would accept her conversion and marriage if it was truly her free will, and urged the court to examine whether coercion influenced conversion and Nikah.

April 18, 2012

Registrar Statement & Outcome

Rinkle, Dr. Lata, and Aasha appeared in Supreme Court. Chief Justice directed statements to be recorded in Registrar’s office. Rinkle handed a paper to the Chief Justice; its content was not made public. Order was written before statements were taken, and girls were instructed to record statements after a short interval, but they were reportedly called later. Reports claimed Mitho’s side met the Registrar and individuals were present during recording. Registrar’s issued statement later said Rinkle opted to go with her “husband.”

May 17, 2012

Petition Disposed (PHC Forced Conversions)

While disposing of the Pakistan Hindu Council petition on forced conversions, the Chief Justice stated that due to Article 20 of the Constitution, there was no need for special legislation for protection of minority rights.

May 22, 2012

Review Petition Filed

Family filed a Review Petition in Supreme Court, urging an inquiry into the role of Ghotki police and Mian Mitho in the case.

Why This Case Matters

Systemic Failure

Exposed deep cracks in the judicial protection of vulnerable minorities, where influence often outweighs justice.

Minority Persecution

Represents the targeted pattern of forced conversions affecting Hindu and Christian girls across Sindh and Punjab.

Global Awareness

Sparked international debate and calls for legislative reform to set a minimum age for marriage and conversion.
